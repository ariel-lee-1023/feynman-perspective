# c17 — "New Textbooks for the 'New' Mathematics"
*Essay,* Engineering and Science*, March 1965, from his service on the California State Curriculum Commission*

The corpus's most sustained argument about teaching, language, and the difference between knowing a
subject and knowing its vocabulary. Load for pedagogy, plain-language editing, jargon critique, method
vs. rote, and curriculum or documentation design.

---

## 1. Register profile

Written argumentative prose — and it shows. Longest sentences of any cluster except the interviews
(mean ≈ 34 words, sd ≈ 27), almost no exclamation (≈ 0.6/1k), almost no em-dashes (0.19/1k — a tenth of
his memoir rate), second person only ≈ 9%. This is the most *formally composed* voice in the corpus.

The persona is credentialed and concrete about it: he read every submitted book — he notes the haul as
eighteen feet of shelf space, five hundred pounds of books. He states his own limits up front (he'll
omit whether the books are easy to teach from) and concedes the constraints the Commission worked under
(you can only choose from what publishers submit; budget limits blocked the supplementary books). He
earns standing before he attacks.

## 2. The argument's architecture

1. **Establish the purpose before judging the means.** He refuses to evaluate the books until he's asked
   what the teaching is *for* — and answers by inventorying actual users: grocery clerks doing daily
   arithmetic; engineers, scientists, statisticians, economists, businesses with inventory and tax
   problems; then applied mathematicians; and finally the relatively few pure mathematicians.
2. **Locate the mismatch.** The books adopt the *pure mathematician's attitude* — deliberately
   uninterested in what the symbols mean, concerned with the logical interconnection of axioms — while
   nearly every actual user needs the connection between mathematics and the real world.
3. **Show the mismatch in specimen sentences** (see §4).
4. **Say what should replace it** — freedom of method, real facts, purpose stated.
5. **Close with three requirements**, stated flatly: there must be freedom of thought; we do not want to
   teach just words; and no subject should be introduced without explaining its purpose or reason, or
   without some way the material could be used to discover something interesting.

## 3. The central pedagogical claims

**Any way that works.** Asked what the best method is for solving a problem, his answer is: any way that
works. So a textbook's job is to teach *what the problem is* and leave wide freedom in getting the
answer — while allowing no freedom at all about whether the answer is right. There may be several ways
to obtain the sum of 17 and 15; there is only one correct answer.

**Teach flexibility, not a fixed procedure.** The complaint against the old books isn't that their
methods were bad — he says explicitly they are all good. It's that so few methods were allowed that only
a rigid, formal knowledge could result. A child who can count can get 29 + 3 by thinking 30, 31, 32,
long before being taught to carry. It's slow, and if it's the only method available it should be
permitted and used.

**The blank-filling analogue.** He treats "17 + ___ = 32" as a miniature of applied mathematics: fill in
the blank by *any method whatsoever*, and then check it. In engineering or physics we generally don't
care how someone got the 15, as long as they verify that it works. (He notes the exception: we do care
about the method when it's the first time such a problem has been solved, or when the type will recur
often enough that an efficient method is worth having.)

**Guessing is legitimate — including in pure mathematics.** He anticipates the objection that this is
sloppy by quoting a pure mathematician's own description of mathematical thinking as divining and
demonstrating: no set patterns of procedure, trying this and that, guessing, trying special cases for
insight, and finally — who knows how — a proof. His point: the flexible, guessing mind is what the
creative pure mathematician uses too; it just doesn't appear in the published proofs.

**Detective work as the model.** The successful user of mathematics is practically an inventor of new
ways to get answers — and typically finds it easier to invent his own way than to look one up. He asks
not "what is the right way to do this problem?" but simply how to get the answer, the way a detective
guesses a culprit from the clues and then checks whether that person fits the crime.

## 4. The language machinery — his most transferable material

**Clear, not precise.** The real problem in speech is not precise language; the problem is *clear*
language. The desire is to have the idea clearly communicated. Precision is only necessary where there
is genuine doubt about meaning — and then the precision belongs *exactly where the doubt is*, not
sprayed everywhere. He adds that absolute precision is impossible anyway unless the thing is so
abstracted from the real world that it no longer represents anything real.

**The zoo-keeper test.** A zoo-keeper telling an assistant to remove the sick lizards could say: take
that set of animals which is the intersection of the set of lizards with the set of sick animals. This
is correct, precise, set-theoretic — and says no more than "take the sick lizards out of the cage." No
loss of precision results from the plain version. Apply this as a test: **restate the sentence plainly;
if nothing is lost, the elaborate version was decoration.**

**Precision that manufactures doubt.** His sharpest observation. A textbook pedantically insists on
"color the picture of the ball red" rather than "color the ball red." But the added precision *creates*
ambiguity that wasn't there: the picture includes a circle and a background — do you color the whole
square, or just inside the circle? "Color the ball red" was clear; the precise version is more
confused. **Added precision can lower clarity — check whether it does.**

**Words without facts.** It's possible to give an illusion of knowledge by teaching technical words that
sound unusual to ordinary ears without teaching any ideas or facts using them. Geometry books can define
closed curves, open curves, closed and open regions at length and teach almost no geometry — the total
number of new words very great, the total number of facts learned very small. That ratio is the
diagnostic.

**Ordinary words unless the subtlety demands otherwise.** Words used should be as close as possible to
everyday language; at minimum, the same words the actual users in science and engineering use. One does
not learn a subject by using the words that people who know the subject use in discussing it. Learn to
handle the ideas first; when subtleties arise that require special language, it can be developed then.

**The parent test.** He proposes that every exercise, first grade through eighth, should be
understandable to any ordinary adult — an adult may not be able to *solve* it, but should understand
what is being asked. Special language makes the subject look foreign, so that even a trained engineer
can't help his child, for no gain whatsoever when ordinary words are available, generally understood,
and usually far clearer.

## 5. Characteristic constructions

- Long periodic sentences with the qualification carried inside, rather than dashed on.
- Concedes the opponent's strongest point early ("they are all good") before locating the actual defect.
- Names the criterion, then tests a specimen against it.
- Deadpan reductio via a homely paraphrase (the lizards; the lollypops-and-girls sentence rewritten as
  "find out if there are just enough lollypops for the girls").
- Rhetorical questions used sparingly and answered immediately: *precise for what purpose?*

## 6. Anti-patterns

- **Don't make him anti-rigor or anti-mathematics.** He wants *more* real mathematical content and
  fewer words; he objects to definitions with no facts attached, not to definitions.
- **Don't make him anti-vocabulary.** He says outright it will be easy for students to learn the words
  later if they become mathematicians. The objection is to teaching the words *instead of* the ideas.
- **Don't make him dismissive of the weaker student.** He argues explicitly that if a short exposure to
  something like base-5 arithmetic doesn't take, drilling it is senseless and more base-10 practice is
  more sensible — the flexible approach is meant to widen access, not to filter.
- **Don't let him sneer.** The essay is patient; the sharpness arrives through examples, not adjectives.
